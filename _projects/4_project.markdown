Chagas disease is a vector-borne illness affecting around 6 to 8 million people world wide. Caused by the hemoflagellate protist species Trypaosoma cruzi, the disease is the leading cause of infectious myocarditis and is a prominant cause of cardiovascular death in endemic areas. The insect vector, Triatoma spp. (also known as kissing bugs), are common across the Americas with the highest species diversity in North America found in Texas. Recently, strategies for parasite control have included investigations of the bacterial gut microbiome in insect vectors opening up the possibility of applying these methods to T. cruzi control in triatomine insects. The natural diversity of gut microbiota across natural populations of triatomine species has yet not been explored in depth. Documenting the impact of T. cruzi on the diversity of the triatomine gut microbiome as well as shifts in microbiota across different species may illuminate possible microbial strategies to control Chagas disease.

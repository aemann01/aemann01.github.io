The primate gut is home to trillions of microorganisms from all kingdoms of life (and viruses too!). The assembly, structure, and stability of these microbial communities is shaped by host gut physiology, diet, phylogeny, host age, geography, and social behaviors. Our current understanding of gut microbial communities is shaped by a disproportionate focus on the role of bacteria but micro- and macro-eukaryotes also can shape the community through predation, resource competition, and interaction with the host immune system. Wild non-human primates are a valuable study system for understanding how eukaryotes have shaped the evolution and ecology of the gut microbiome as they occupy diverse environments, have a variety of diets, and live in social gropus that range in both size and complexity. Additionally, as our closest living relatives, non-human primates provide an important evolutionary context for understanding the modern human gut microbiome. As human groups have shifted into more urban and industrial lifestyles, there has been a substantial loss of microbial diversity in the gut (including gut eukaryotes) which has been linked to several chronic diseases. Documenting the natural diversity of microbial eukaryotes in wild non-human primate groups can help us understand the mechanisms and consequences of this loss.
